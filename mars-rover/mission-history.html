Mission History

  • 2004: Opportunity lands at Meridiani Planum to begin a 90-sol mission.
  • 2005: Finds hematite “blueberries” — evidence of past water.
  • 2012: Curiosity lands in Gale Crater using sky crane system.
  • 2014: Curiosity finds clay minerals formed in water.
  • 2018: Contact with Opportunity lost after a Martian dust storm.
  • 2023: Curiosity still exploring and detecting methane spikes.

Opportunity Rover

Mission Goals and Overview

Opportunity was part of NASA's Mars Exploration Rover mission, designed to search for and characterize rocks and soils indicating past water activity. Expected to operate for 90 sols, it functioned for nearly 15 years.

Launch and Landing Details

  • Launch Date: July 7, 2003
  • Launch Vehicle: Delta II rocket
  • Landing Date: January 25, 2004
  • Landing Site: Meridiani Planum

Major Discoveries or Accomplishments

  • First direct evidence of past liquid water on Mars.
  • Traveled over 28 miles—setting a Martian record.
  • Discovered hematite “blueberries” that form in water.

Key Technical or Design Features

  • Solar-powered, six-wheeled rover.
  • Equipped with Pancam, rock abrasion tool, and spectrometers.
  • Operated for 5,111 sols until a dust storm in 2018.

Curiosity Rover

Mission Goals and Overview

Curiosity is part of NASA's Mars Science Laboratory mission. Its goal is to assess whether Mars ever had conditions suitable for life. It continues to explore Mars' climate and geology.

Launch and Landing Details

  • Launch Date: November 26, 2011
  • Launch Vehicle: Atlas V rocket
  • Landing Date: August 6, 2012
  • Landing Site: Gale Crater

Major Discoveries or Accomplishments

  • Found ancient lakebeds and clay minerals.
  • Detected organic molecules and methane variations.
  • Provided high-res photos and atmospheric readings.

Key Technical or Design Features

  • Nuclear-powered with a long mission lifespan.
  • Equipped with ChemCam laser and onboard lab.
  • Has advanced wheels and suspension for rough terrain.
