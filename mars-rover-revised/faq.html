Frequently Asked Questions: Curiosity & Opportunity

1. What is the Curiosity rover?

Curiosity is a car-sized rover launched by NASA in 2011 to explore Gale Crater on Mars. It landed in August 2012 and continues to study Mars’ climate and geology.

2. What did Curiosity discover on Mars?

Curiosity found organic molecules, evidence of ancient lakes, methane gas variations, and clay-rich soil — all of which suggest Mars may have once supported microbial life.

3. What powers the Curiosity rover?

Curiosity uses a nuclear-powered MMRTG (Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator), which gives it a long-lasting energy source for extended missions.

4. What is the Opportunity rover?

Opportunity was one of NASA’s twin Mars Exploration Rovers, launched in 2003 and landed in 2004. It vastly outlived its planned mission, operating for nearly 15 years.

5. What were Opportunity’s biggest discoveries?

Opportunity discovered hematite spheres ("blueberries"), signs of past water activity, and evidence that Mars had wet conditions in its distant past.

6. How did Opportunity get its power?

Opportunity was powered by solar panels. It eventually stopped communicating after a massive dust storm in 2018 blocked its sunlight.

7. Are either of the rovers still active?

Curiosity is still active and exploring Mars today. Opportunity's mission ended in 2018 after it lost power, but it sent back valuable data over its long lifespan.
